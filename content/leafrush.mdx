## The service

<TLDR>A done-for-you agency for small lawn-care businesses, with two core deliverables: a custom website and a fully managed Google Ads account.</TLDR>

I targeted small to mid-sized lawn-care and landscaping companies. Once a client
onboarded, the engagement ran on a standardized system I built so I could take on
several clients at once:

- **Operator playbook.** A Google Drive with five short guides on the fundamentals
  that actually move the needle for a local service business: quoting within five
  minutes of a lead, how to ask for a review, and similar basics that are simple
  but decisive.
- **Website.** A Google Form collected every detail I needed, then a four-phase,
  multi-week build on WordPress and Elementor. About 70% of it was custom code,
  because I modularized the build so I could ship sites fast and run several
  clients in parallel.
- **Google Ads.** Four to five days building and standardizing each client's
  account from scratch.

The website and the Google Ads account were the two things clients were really
paying for.

## Origin: from middleman to operator

<TLDR>I started by white-labeling the work, but the team didn't know lawn care, so I learned the entire craft myself to own the outcome.</TLDR>

I started as the middleman, hiring a white-label team to do the delivery. They were
good, but their experience was with larger businesses and bigger ad budgets, and
they weren't specialized in lawn care, so they missed the nuances the industry
carries. They underperformed, and communication was a mess. I didn't fully
understand their terminology, which meant I often couldn't even relay to the client
what was going on. So I decided to learn it myself, end to end. Owning the delivery
was the only way to own the result.

## Building the machine

<TLDR>Once I was the bottleneck, I outsourced cold calling first, then site production, so the business could grow without every task routing through me.</TLDR>

Learning the craft created a new problem: I became the bottleneck. I hit a ceiling
where I couldn't grow, because all my time went to managing, creating, and
optimizing accounts, with nothing left for outreach. So I built the business out
around me, with SOPs and contractors. I outsourced cold calling first to refill the
pipeline, then brought on help to produce the websites. By the end it ran on a team
of five.

## The hard part: winning on ultra-tiny budgets

<TLDR>Sub-$1K monthly budgets locked me out of automated bidding, so I won on total manual control, tight copy-to-keyword-to-page alignment, and hyper-focused location campaigns.</TLDR>

The real challenge was the delivery itself, and it was brutal. My clients' budgets
were in what the Google Ads community calls the ultra-tiny range, under $1,000 a
month. That locked me out of every automated tool: no AI bidding, no optimizing for
conversions, none of it works with that little data. And I was competing against
monsters like TruGreen and Augusta Lawn Care, who were cheaper and outspent me, in
a service that is close to perfectly competitive.

What worked:

- **Total manual control** over every campaign, ad, and keyword. No automation to
  lean on.
- **Tight alignment** across ad copy, keyword, and landing page, so every click
  landed somewhere that matched its intent.
- **Differentiation on quality and care, not price**, because I could never win the
  price war against the giants.
- **Hyper-focused location campaigns** instead of campaigns split by service, a
  structure that concentrated tiny budgets where they could actually convert.

## The mechanism behind 0 to $100K

<TLDR>Several clients reached $100K and averaged a 230% acquisition lift on the same repeatable blueprint: Google Business Profile, Google Ads, and the website working together.</TLDR>

Several clients reached $100K in revenue, and across clients the blueprint drove an
average 230% lift in acquisition. It came from a repeatable system rather than one
lucky break. The combination was Google Business Profile
management, Google Ads, and the website, working together: get reviews, drive
traffic, convert it, repeat. Once the system worked for one lawn-care business, the
same blueprint transferred to the next.

## Experimentation as the engine

<TLDR>Over four years I ran 200+ A/B tests across landing pages, ads, campaigns, and even my own outreach, and prioritized by the metric that mattered, not the vanity one.</TLDR>

Underneath the delivery system, the real engine was constant experimentation. Over
four years I ran more than 200 A/B tests across every surface that touched revenue:
landing pages, ad copy and creative, campaign structure, website design, and even
my own outreach funnels (cold email and cold-calling scripts).

The judgment was in *which metric to optimize*. A change that lifted click-through
rate often hurt conversion rate or cost-per-acquisition, so I learned to optimize
for the metric that actually moved a client's profit, not the one that looked good
in a dashboard. Choosing to sacrifice CTR to protect CVR or CPA is the same call a
product team makes when it resists a change that boosts a vanity engagement number
but erodes the core outcome.

This is the most product-shaped work I did at Leafrush: form a hypothesis, run the
experiment, read the metric honestly, and decide, on repeat, at volume.

## What it taught me

<TLDR>Clients only care about results, not your process, and in a busy season the scarce skill is prioritizing by value, effort, and urgency.</TLDR>

- **Clients care about outcomes, not effort.** They do not care what you do, how
  you do it, or why. They care whether it is profitable or whether they are losing
  money. Building toward the number the customer actually feels is the instinct I
  carry into product.
- **Prioritization is the real job at scale.** In spring I got buried under existing
  accounts and new clients. I could do a hundred genuinely productive things a day,
  so the skill became choosing the few that mattered most, weighing value against
  effort and urgency rather than treating every task as equal.
- **Pricing is a lever, not a label.** Restructuring how I priced engagements added
  25% to my margins without adding a single client, a reminder that what you charge
  is a deliberate decision you can revisit, not a number you set once.
- **Scale strains quality.** Growing client count put real pressure on delivery
  quality, and I learned that protecting the standard sometimes means growing
  slower on purpose.

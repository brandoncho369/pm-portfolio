<Story
  title="The service"
  hook={`A done-for-you agency for small lawn-care businesses, with two core deliverables: a custom website and a fully managed Google Ads account.`}
>

I targeted small to mid-sized lawn-care and landscaping companies. Once a client
onboarded, the engagement ran on a **standardized system** I built so I could run
several clients at once:

- **Operator playbook.** Five short guides on the fundamentals that move the needle
  for a local service business: quoting within five minutes of a lead, how to ask
  for a review, simple but decisive basics.
- **Website.** A four-phase build on WordPress and Elementor, about **70% custom
  code**, modularized so I could ship sites fast and run clients in parallel.
- **Google Ads.** Four to five days building and standardizing each account from
  scratch.

The **website and the Google Ads account** were what clients were really paying for.

</Story>

<Story
  title="Origin: from middleman to operator"
  hook={`I started by white-labeling the work, but the team didn't know lawn care, so I learned the entire craft myself to own the outcome.`}
>

I started as the middleman, hiring a **white-label team** to deliver. Their
experience was big-budget general agencies, not lawn care, so they missed the
industry's nuances and underperformed. Worse, I couldn't translate their jargon
back to clients, so communication broke down.

**So I learned the entire craft myself, end to end.** Owning the delivery was the
only way to own the result.

</Story>

<Story
  title="Building the machine"
  hook={`Once I was the bottleneck, I outsourced cold calling first, then site production, so the business could grow without every task routing through me.`}
>

Learning the craft created a new problem: **I became the bottleneck.** Every
account, build, and optimization ran through me, leaving no time for outreach, so
growth stalled. The fix was to build the business *around* me, with SOPs and
contractors:

- **Outsourced cold calling first**, to refill the pipeline.
- **Then site production**, so builds no longer depended on me.

By the end it ran on a **team of five.**

</Story>

<Story
  title="The hard part: winning on ultra-tiny budgets"
  hook={`Sub-$1K monthly budgets locked me out of automated bidding, so I won on total manual control, tight copy-to-keyword-to-page alignment, and hyper-focused location campaigns.`}
>

The challenge was the delivery itself, and it was brutal. My clients' budgets were
**sub-$1,000 a month**, what the Google Ads community calls "ultra-tiny." That
locked me out of every automated tool (AI bidding, conversion optimization, all of
which need more data), while I competed against giants like **TruGreen and Augusta
Lawn Care** who were cheaper and outspent me.

**What worked:**

- **Total manual control** over every campaign, ad, and keyword.
- **Tight alignment** across ad copy, keyword, and landing page, so every click
  matched its intent.
- **Quality and care over price**, because I could never win the price war.
- **Hyper-focused location campaigns** instead of by-service campaigns,
  concentrating tiny budgets where they actually converted.

</Story>

<Story
  title="The mechanism behind 0 to $100K"
  hook={`Several clients reached $100K and averaged a 230% acquisition lift on the same repeatable blueprint: Google Business Profile, Google Ads, and the website working together.`}
>

Several clients reached **$100K in revenue** on a repeatable system, not one lucky
break, and the blueprint averaged a **230% lift in acquisition**. It was Google
Business Profile, Google Ads, and the website working as one loop:

<Callout title="The repeatable blueprint">
Get reviews → drive traffic → convert → repeat. Once it worked for one lawn-care business, the same loop transferred to the next.
</Callout>

</Story>

<Story
  title="Experimentation as the engine"
  hook={`Over four years I ran 200+ A/B tests across landing pages, ads, campaigns, and even my own outreach, and prioritized by the metric that mattered, not the vanity one.`}
>

Underneath the delivery system, the real engine was **constant experimentation**.
Over four years I ran **200+ A/B tests** across every revenue surface: landing
pages, ad copy and creative, campaign structure, website design, even my own
cold-email and cold-call funnels.

<Callout title="The signature judgment call">
A change that lifted click-through rate often hurt conversion or cost-per-acquisition. I learned to optimize for the metric that moved a client's *profit*, not the one that looked good in a dashboard. Sacrificing CTR to protect CVR or CPA is the same call a product team makes when it resists a change that spikes a vanity metric but erodes the core outcome.
</Callout>

The most product-shaped work I did at Leafrush: form a hypothesis, run the
experiment, read the metric honestly, decide. On repeat, at volume.

</Story>

<Story
  title="What it taught me"
  hook={`Clients only care about results, not your process, and in a busy season the scarce skill is prioritizing by value, effort, and urgency.`}
>

- **Clients care about outcomes, not effort.** They don't care what you do or how.
  They care whether it's profitable. Building toward the number the customer
  actually feels is the instinct I carry into product.
- **Prioritization is the real job at scale.** In a busy spring I could do a hundred
  productive things a day, so the skill became choosing the few that mattered most,
  by value, effort, and urgency.
- **Pricing is a lever, not a label.** A pricing restructure added **25% margin**
  without adding a single client. What you charge is a decision you revisit, not a
  number you set once.
- **Scale strains quality.** More clients pressured delivery quality, and protecting
  the standard sometimes meant growing slower on purpose.

</Story>

<Callout title="Why this is on a PM portfolio">
Running Leafrush was product management without the title. I owned an outcome end to
end, ran a continuous experimentation program of 200+ A/B tests, made constant
metric-tradeoff and value-versus-effort calls under real constraints, and learned to
obsess over the result the customer actually feels, not the work behind it.
</Callout>

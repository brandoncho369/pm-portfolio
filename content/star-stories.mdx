<Story
  title="Cut a hard dependency before it could sink the product"
  hook={`Moved Tonbo's primary interface off Discord to an owned web dashboard, cutting my single biggest platform dependency without losing the real-time alerts users valued.`}
>

- **Situation:** I built Tonbo, an arbitrage-intelligence tool for the Japanese watch market, and at first ran the entire experience and its alerts through Discord.
- **Task:** As it grew, I had to decide whether Discord should stay the primary interface. It was working, but I was uneasy about depending on a platform I didn't control.
- **Action:** I weighed it honestly. Discord gave me free instant notifications and zero auth to maintain, but it was a single point of failure I didn't own, and it didn't read as credible to users. Rather than go all-or-nothing, I moved the primary interface to a web dashboard with magic-link login, and kept Discord and email for notifications, the one job Discord was genuinely best at.
- **Result:** I removed my biggest dependency risk and gave the product a credible front door, without losing the notification experience users liked.
- **Lesson:** Match each channel to what it is actually good at, and be wary of building on a platform you don't own.

</Story>

<Story
  title="Let user research overrule my own assumptions"
  hook={`About 30 user interviews surfaced a need I never assumed, an automated landed-cost calculator, which I prioritized over my own roadmap and it became the feature users pointed to.`}
>

- **Situation:** Starting Tonbo, I had my own assumptions about what mattered most, mostly alerting and comp data.
- **Task:** Before building the wrong things, I wanted to validate what users actually needed.
- **Action:** I ran about 30 interviews with flippers, dealers, and collectors sourced through Reddit. The most useful finding was something I had not assumed at all: unprompted, people kept asking for a way to estimate true landed cost, because taxes, shipping, and import fees are confusing and made them hesitant to act. It was a clear, repeated signal, so I prioritized and shipped an automated landed-cost calculator even though it was not on my roadmap.
- **Result:** Testers adopted it quickly and called it out as a standout. It became a feature people pointed to.
- **Lesson:** My gut about what mattered was wrong. The highest-impact feature came from a pattern across real users, not from me.

</Story>

<Story
  title="Communicate uncertainty instead of faking confidence"
  hook={`Added a low-to-high confidence score to Tonbo's price comps so users could tell a well-backed deal from a thin one, protecting both their trust and their money.`}
>

- **Situation:** A core part of Tonbo matches US price comps to Japanese listings to compute margin, but the comp data was not uniform. Some pieces had solid data, others just one or two data points.
- **Task:** I realized that presenting every estimate as equally reliable would mislead users into over-trusting margins built on thin data, and they could lose money.
- **Action:** Rather than hide the uncertainty or pretend the data was cleaner than it was, I built a low-to-high confidence score onto the comps and alerts. Users could tell a high-confidence deal backed by many sales from a shaky one based on a couple of data points, and make their own informed call.
- **Result:** Users could weigh deals appropriately instead of treating thin estimates as sure things, which protected their trust in the product.
- **Lesson:** With any data or prediction product, communicating how confident the system is can matter as much as the prediction itself.

</Story>

<Story
  title="Resolve a co-founder disagreement by finding the real need"
  hook={`Turned a co-founder standoff over drill explanations into a shipped fix by separating the underlying need from the proposed solution: an expandable read-more layer that fit our constraints.`}
>

- **Situation:** On Poker Ascent, the training app I co-founded, my co-founder and I disagreed about the core drills. He wanted much deeper and more varied explanations on each one.
- **Task:** I had to resolve it. He had a legitimate point about user value, but there were two real constraints: our drill engine was not built for that depth, and long explanations would break the UI.
- **Action:** Instead of shutting it down on technical grounds or over-building something that would wreck the experience, I separated what he actually wanted from how he proposed getting it. The underlying need was that users understand the reasoning, and that explanations not feel repetitive. So I shipped a fix that fit our constraints: an expandable "read more" layer that kept the default UI clean, plus rewritten copy that read as more varied.
- **Result:** It resolved the disagreement, he felt genuinely heard, and we shipped something that served the need without breaking the engine or cluttering the UI.
- **Lesson:** Most disagreements are about the solution, not the goal. If you dig to the underlying need, you can usually find an option that honors it within your constraints.

</Story>

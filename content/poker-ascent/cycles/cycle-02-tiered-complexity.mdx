## Summary (BLUF)

Our engine models poker realistically (playstyle, player count, stack sizes,
villain archetypes, position, hand, board texture, and prior streets), and
beginners were overwhelmed by having to weigh all of it at once. We introduced a
five-tier leveling system that introduces variables gradually (tiers 1 to 4),
capped by an open-ended full-complexity mode (tier 5). 14 of 22 beginners reached
tier 3 within their first 7 sessions, and zero experts dropped off in that window.

## Trigger & problem

<TLDR>The realism that makes the engine credible was the same thing drowning new users, who couldn't weigh every variable at once.</TLDR>

The engine accounts for everything that genuinely matters in a real hand: intended
playstyle, heads-up vs. multiway, your stack and villains' stacks, villain
archetypes (nit, loose, etc.), position, the hand, board texture, and previous
streets. That realism is the point, but it is a lot to hold at once. Beginners
told us it was too complicated: they didn't know how to weigh each variable, and
the volume of information left them overloaded and confused. The realism we needed
for credibility was the same thing drowning new users.

## Discovery & user-research synthesis

<TLDR>Every variable is a real input to a correct decision, so the problem wasn't what we modeled, it was how much we exposed at once.</TLDR>

The complaint came from the same beta cohort, concentrated again in beginners:
they couldn't prioritize among the variables and felt overwhelmed by the full set
presented at once. Crucially, the variables aren't noise. Every one is a real,
necessary input to a correct decision, so the problem wasn't *what* we modeled, it
was *how much we exposed at once* to a user still learning the fundamentals.

Of the 22 beginners, 8 explicitly reported feeling overwhelmed, and we expect the
real number is higher, since others may simply not have said anything.

## The PRD

<TLDR>A tiered system that introduces variables progressively, gated by earned XP, so beginners learn gradually and experts aren't slowed to a crawl.</TLDR>

<PRD>

### Problem
Beginners are overwhelmed by the full variable set and can't weigh inputs
appropriately.

### Target user
Beginners (must learn gradually) and experts (must not be slowed to a crawl).

### Success metric (defined upfront)
Beginner progression through the early tiers.

### Guardrails
1. Learning quality holds at full complexity.
2. Expert drop-off doesn't worsen.

### In scope
- A tiered system that introduces variables progressively, gated by earned XP.

### Out of scope
- Hiding variables permanently.
- Free tier-selection.

### Constraint
- Content must support months-long retention, not be exhausted in weeks.

</PRD>

## Prioritization

<TLDR>Chose a tiered leveling system over hiding variables or a settings toggle, because scaffolding teaches and accuracy-driven XP lets skill pace the climb.</TLDR>

- **Keep some variables implicit or behind the scenes.** Reduces load, but this is
  an education product. Users *should* learn that these factors exist and matter.
  Hiding them undercuts the core value. Rejected.
- **Let users pick a complexity level in settings.** Flexible, but users forget to
  change it. They would either stay stuck on easy and never progress, or jump up
  and feel overwhelmed all over again. It also offloads a teaching decision onto
  the learner. Rejected.
- **Tiered leveling system (chosen).** Five tiers. Tiers 1 to 4 each introduce one
  or a few new variables. **Tier 5 is an open-ended "infinite" mode at full
  complexity**, so tier 4 is the last tier of *added* complexity. Higher tiers
  require more drill completions to clear, and XP is earned through accuracy.

**Why it wins:** beginners learn when it is simple and add variables one layer at a
time as they level up. It is scaffolding, not a settings toggle they will forget.
Progression is structured, so there is a real sense of advancement and completion.
And because XP is accuracy-driven, *skill itself paces the climb*.

## Build & sprint summary

<TLDR>Built the five tiers plus a hot-streak XP mechanic, where consecutive correct answers accelerate the climb so skill self-calibrates speed.</TLDR>

Five-tier progression. Each of tiers 1 to 4 introduces new variable(s). Tier 5 is
full-complexity infinite mode. XP is awarded for accuracy, and clearing higher
tiers requires more completions. We deliberately gated content to support
**months-long retention**. The app shouldn't be finishable (and deletable) in a
single month.

**Intervention for the expert problem, a hot-streak XP mechanic:** consecutive
correct answers accelerate XP gain. This lets skill self-calibrate speed: the more
accurate you are, the faster you clear the low tiers, without a separate placement
step. (Note this is the mechanic we built, not a metric. See Measurement.)

## Measurement & retro

<TLDR>14 of 22 beginners reached tier 3 in 7 sessions with zero early expert drop-off, but the learning-quality guardrail lives at tier 5 and is honestly months out.</TLDR>

This feature has to do two things at once, cut beginner overwhelm *and* preserve
learning without driving off experts, so no single metric carries it. The
guardrails do as much work as the primary.

**Primary, honest framing:** this is an *absolute* progression figure for a system
that didn't exist before, not a before/after delta. On its own it shows that most
beginners advanced, not that overwhelm fell. The overwhelm-symptom metric dropping
and learning quality holding are what close that loop.

**Guardrail 2 (expert churn), caveat:** experts are proxied as users posting high
accuracy from their first drills. The hot-streak mechanic was the intervention,
and zero early quits is the signal it didn't backfire. Over a 7-session window,
"no churn" and "not-yet-churned" look identical. This shows we didn't bleed
experts *early*, not that the long-grind problem is solved.

**Guardrail 1 (learning quality):** defined as accuracy on full-complexity drills,
but not yet readable in this window by design. Full complexity lives at tier 5,
which no one reached within 7 days because tiers gate content for months-long
retention. We defined the right guardrail. Honestly, the measurement is months
out, not a 7-session result.

**Secondary and diagnostic signals worth instrumenting:**

- Overwhelm-feedback frequency (confirms the symptom, doesn't prove the cure).
- Per-variable "dip-and-recover" accuracy when a new variable is introduced (the
  signature of scaffolding actually working).
- Time or completions to clear each tier.

### Retro & lessons

Two tensions we are watching, not smoothing over:

- **Retention vs. experts.** Months-long gated content is good for retention but is
  exactly what risks experts later. Our zero-churn number simply hasn't reached
  the grind danger-zone yet. Hot-streak makes experts climb *faster*, not *fewer*
  tiers. They still play every low tier.
- **Expert-fix vs. learning-quality.** Accelerating XP on streaks pushes *everyone*
  accurate forward faster, including a beginner on an easy run who hasn't
  consolidated, which could shove them back into overwhelm. So guardrails 1 and 2
  must be watched together, not in isolation.

**Next iteration:** a calibrated entry point, a short placement or skill assessment
that seeds a user's starting tier, so experts test into higher tiers instead of
grinding low ones while progression stays intact for everyone. It complements
hot-streak (which fixes *speed*) by fixing *whether they should be in the low tiers
at all*. This is the cycle where the portfolio's recurring principle, *don't tax
users who don't need it*, was hardest to hold, and the entry point is how we would
honor it without sacrificing progression.

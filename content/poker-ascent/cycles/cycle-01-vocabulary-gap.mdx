## Summary (BLUF)

Beginner testers couldn't understand the poker terms inside our own explanations,
which broke the core learn loop for exactly the users the product exists to serve.
We shipped a two-layer fix: a Study-tab glossary plus inline, clickable term
definitions that surface at the moment of confusion. In-session "what does this
mean?" questions from beginners dropped to effectively zero.

## Trigger & problem

<TLDR>Our explanations taught using jargon beginners didn't know yet, so the moment meant to teach was the moment that lost them.</TLDR>

Poker Ascent's core value is a learn loop: a player misses a decision, gets an
explanation, and learns from it. But the explanations themselves leaned on poker
jargon (*pot odds, fold equity, reverse implied odds*) that beginners didn't yet
know. So the moment that was supposed to teach was the moment that lost them. The
teaching mechanism was failing at the exact point it was meant to work.

## Discovery & user-research synthesis

<TLDR>17 of 22 beginners flagged the same thing: they couldn't follow the terminology inside the explanations.</TLDR>

Our first beta cohort was **40 college poker players** spanning beginner to
experienced, all people we knew personally and played with. After the first round
of testing, one complaint dominated among beginners: **17 of 22 beginners** raised
the terminology in our explanations.

<Quote author="Beginner tester" role="first beta cohort">
Dude, I don't know what this means. What's reverse implied odds?
</Quote>

The load-bearing insight is in the segmentation: the breakage was concentrated in
**beginners, our target learners**, and it sat *inside the core loop*, not at the
edges. An education product that loses its least-experienced users in the act of
educating has a value-execution gap, not a polish gap.

## The PRD

<TLDR>Repair the loop in context with a glossary plus inline term definitions, without taxing the experienced half of the cohort.</TLDR>

<PRD>

### Problem
Beginners can't follow our explanations because they assume vocabulary beginners
don't have.

### Target user
Beginner players (roughly half the cohort). Experienced players already know the
terms.

### Success metric (defined upfront)
Reduce beginner "what does X mean?" questions during drills. Target: an 80%
decrease.

### In scope
- A glossary reference plus in-context term definitions.

### Out of scope
- A mandatory vocabulary walkthrough.
- A general-purpose LLM tutor.

### Edge cases
- A term that isn't yet in the glossary.
- Experienced players who don't want interruptions (handled by opt-in design).

### Constraints
- Small team, beginner-heavy cohort. Keep it within scope and near-zero marginal
  cost.

</PRD>

## Prioritization

<TLDR>Chose two-layer just-in-time learning: fix the loop in context, opt-in, at near-zero cost, instead of front-loading or off-loading the fix.</TLDR>

- **Mandatory vocabulary walkthrough after onboarding.** Front-loads the cost onto
  everyone, lengthens onboarding, and punishes the roughly half of users who are
  experienced and already know the terms. It taxes all users for a
  beginner-specific problem.
- **A constrained ChatGPT/Claude term-explainer.** Flexible, but it costs money per
  call and risks scope creep. A "just explain terms" tutor quietly becomes a
  chatbot to maintain.
- **Two-layer just-in-time learning (chosen).**
  1. **Inline, underlined, clickable terms** inside drills. Tap any piece of
     jargon for a short popup explanation, delivered at the exact moment of
     confusion, inside the loop.
  2. **A dedicated Study tab.** A thorough glossary players can browse to look up
     or refresh terms on their own time.

**Why it wins:** it repairs the broken loop in context rather than bolting on a
separate lesson, costs effectively nothing per use, and is opt-in by design.
Experienced players simply don't click, so beginners get help without adding any
friction for advanced users.

## Measurement & retro

<TLDR>Beginner in-session questions dropped to effectively zero, but the honest gap is that we didn't track glossary or popup adoption, which is what would prove it.</TLDR>

Because we knew all 40 testers personally, we directly observed and counted "what
does X mean?" questions in sessions.

<MetricResult
  metric="Beginner 'what does X mean?' questions during drills"
  target="80% decrease"
  result="Dropped to effectively zero (observed in sessions)"
  hit={true}
  guardrail="Watch that the inline underlines don't clutter the experience for experienced players"

**Honest framing:** this was observed in sessions on a small, personally-known
cohort, not instrumented analytics, and not a statistically powered sample.

**The gap (caught in retro):** we did **not** track glossary opens or inline-popup
clicks. That usage data is exactly what would *validate* the result. It is the
difference between "questions stopped because beginners self-served the answer"
(success) and "questions stopped because beginners disengaged" (failure). On its
own, "questions went to zero" can't distinguish the two. Adoption tracking isn't a
nice-to-have here. It is the metric that proves the primary signal.

**Guardrail worth adding:** did the clickable underlines distract or clutter the
experience for *experienced* players (half the cohort)? The opt-in design intends
no, but a quick check (do advanced users find the markup noisy?) would confirm we
didn't trade beginner help for advanced-player friction.

### Retro & lessons

Deliver help at the moment of need, in context, and opt-in. Repair the loop where
it breaks instead of front-loading or off-loading the fix, and don't tax the users
who don't need it.

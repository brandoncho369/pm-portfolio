> **Scope note:** a low-priority polish fix, written up short on purpose. Included
> because right-sizing effort to impact, and not dressing a small fix as a saga, is
> part of the job.

## What happened

After cycle 1 shipped the Study-tab glossary, users found it was one long column of
rows and didn't want to scroll hunting for the term they wanted. The content was
already complete. It just wasn't navigable.

## Options considered

- **A to Z jump bar.** Fast, but only helps if you know the term's exact name,
  which beginners often don't.
- **Collapsible accordion sections.** Tidier, but still leaves people hunting.
- **Surface most-viewed or recent terms at the top.** Aids discovery, not directed
  lookup.
- **Full tag or filter system.** Overkill for a glossary.
- **Chosen: a search bar plus category grouping** (positions, poker math, and so
  on) with a category dropdown at the top.

**Why it wins:** it serves both retrieval modes at once. Search for "I know the
term I want," and the category dropdown for "show me what exists in poker math."
One cheap change covers known-item lookup and browsing.

## Build

A search bar at the top of the Study tab, plus terms grouped into categories with a
dropdown to filter.

## Measurement

Deliberately light. Over-instrumenting a search bar would show worse judgment than
the fix itself.

- **Primary, retrieval-tool adoption:** the share of glossary visits that use
  search or the category filter rather than scrolling. This also **closes a thread
  from cycle 1**, where the retro flagged we weren't tracking glossary engagement
  at all. Here we finally are.
- **Guardrail, search responsiveness:** query latency around 100ms, so the tool
  itself adds no friction.
- **Diagnostic and roadmap:** most-searched terms and most-used categories.
  Quietly the most valuable output: the terms people search most are the
  highest-confusion concepts, which points straight at where cycle 1's inline
  definitions and our explanations need the most work.

**Two framing notes, so these numbers claim only what they prove:**

- **Coverage is not findability.** Search returns a hit close to 100% of the time,
  but that is near-tautological, because the glossary already contained every
  in-app term after cycle 1. It confirms the index is complete. It doesn't show the
  search bar helped anyone find faster. Adoption of the new tools does that.
- **Latency is not time-to-find.** The 100ms is search-query speed, not how long a
  human takes to locate a term. We didn't cleanly instrument human time-to-find or
  give-up rate, so the practical evidence here is the complaint disappearing plus
  retrieval-tool adoption. Honest for a fix this size, and named rather than
  dressed up.

## Closes

Retires the open thread from cycle 1 (we weren't tracking glossary usage). The two
glossary cycles now bookend cleanly: **cycle 1 built it, and this made it navigable
and finally instrumented it.**

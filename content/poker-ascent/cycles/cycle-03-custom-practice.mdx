## Summary (BLUF)

Advanced and intermediate testers wanted to drill specific variables without
grinding through tiers to reach them, and to revisit basics, but free movement
through the tiers would have broken the progression system from the previous
cycle. We added a separate **custom-practice mode** (toggle any one or more
variables) at the bottom of the skill-selection screen, leaving the tier spine
untouched. 13 of 40 testers returned to it repeatedly, almost all advanced or
intermediate, and structured-tier session share held at 86% (down from 97%) in the
first window.

## Trigger & problem

<TLDR>The tier system that protected beginners had become a cage for stronger players, who couldn't reach hard variables or revisit basics. This cycle answers cycle 2's open question.</TLDR>

The tier system from cycle 2 produced the exact friction we flagged as its open
question: advanced and intermediate players had to grind a lot of drills to reach
the harder, higher-complexity tiers, and there was no way to drop back and
rehearse the basics. The structure that protected beginners had become a cage for
stronger players. **This cycle is the answer to the open question the last one
raised.**

## Discovery & user-research synthesis

<TLDR>12 of 40 testers, skewing advanced and intermediate, wanted to practice the environment they choose now, which the forward-only spine couldn't serve.</TLDR>

**12 of 40 testers** raised this, skewing toward advanced and intermediate
players. The complaint took two shapes:

- *"I have to grind to reach the complexity I actually want to practice."*
- *"I want to go back and drill the basics and there's no way to."*

Both are the same underlying need: practice the environment you choose, now, which
the single forward-only progression spine couldn't serve.

## The PRD

<TLDR>A separate practice surface with per-variable toggles for stronger players, without altering the tier progression path itself.</TLDR>

### 1. Summary
The tier system gave beginners a gentle on-ramp but left stronger players grinding
to reach the complexity they wanted, with no way to revisit basics. This PRD adds
a separate custom-practice mode (per-variable toggles to build any practice
environment) placed at the bottom of skill selection, without altering the tier
progression path. Goal: serve advanced and intermediate players without breaking
the progression system or exposing beginners to overload.

### 2. Problem & Background
The cycle-2 tier system intentionally gates complexity behind earned progression.
For advanced and intermediate players that became friction: they had to grind
drills to reach harder tiers, and had no route back to rehearse fundamentals. The
structure that protected beginners constrained stronger players, the open question
from the prior cycle, now realized.

### 3. Discovery
12 of 40 testers, skewing advanced and intermediate, asked for the ability to set
their own variables. Two forms: wanting to reach hard complexity without grinding,
and wanting to go back and drill basics. Both reduce to one thing: practicing a
self-chosen environment, on demand.

### 4. Goals & Non-Goals
**Goals**

- Let stronger players drill any chosen combination of variables immediately.
- Allow revisiting basics without losing progression.
- Keep the tier progression system intact.
- Keep beginners away from accidental full-complexity overload.

**Non-Goals**

- Changing how the tiers themselves work or unlock.
- Making custom mode the default or primary path.
- Targeting beginners with this feature.

### 5. Success Metrics

- **Primary, retained adoption (target segment):** repeat use of custom mode by
  intermediate and advanced players. (Result: 13 of 40 returned repeatedly, almost
  all advanced or intermediate. 27 one-time trials discounted as launch-bundle
  curiosity.)
- **Guardrail 1, progression holds:** structured-tier share of session time.
  (Result: 97% to 86%. Held in-window, slope to be monitored.)
- **Guardrail 2, beginner overload:** beginner toggling and accuracy when using
  custom mode. (Result: no observed beginner use or misuse. Light instrumentation,
  tighten going forward.)
- **Secondary (diagnostic):** most-toggled variable combinations (a roadmap
  signal), and whether custom-mode users progress faster on the main path.

### 6. Target User
Intermediate and advanced players who want targeted practice or to revisit
fundamentals. Beginners are explicitly out of scope and steered away by placement.

### 7. User Stories

- As an advanced player, I want to drill a specific hard variable now without
  grinding up the tiers, so practice matches what I'm working on.
- As an improving player, I want to go back and rehearse basics, so I can shore up
  weak spots.
- As a beginner, I should not be nudged into a mode that lets me turn on more
  complexity than I can handle.

### 8. Solution Overview
A custom-practice option at the bottom of the skill-selection screen. The player
toggles on one or more variables (position, hand, board texture, intended
playstyle, villain archetype, stack sizes, multiway) to construct the exact
practice environment, then drills it freely. The tier progression path is
untouched and runs in parallel.

Design rationale: rather than loosen the progression structure for everyone, add a
parallel surface for the users who need it. Bottom-of-screen placement keeps it
discoverable for seekers and off the default path for beginners.

### 9. Functional Requirements

- Custom-practice entry appears at the bottom of skill selection.
- Player can toggle any subset of the engine's variables on or off to define the
  environment.
- Drills run against the chosen environment with no effect on tier state or
  progression.
- Selected variable combinations are logged (for the diagnostic metric).

### 10. Edge Cases & Constraints

- Player toggles everything on: full complexity outside the tier path (intended
  for advanced users, monitored via guardrail 2 for beginner misuse).
- No variables toggled: define a disabled state or a sensible default.
- Constraint: must not alter or interfere with tier progression mechanics.

### 11. Risks & Mitigations

- **Cannibalizing progression.** Custom mode could pull users off the structured
  path. Mitigation: it is a parallel supplement, not a replacement. Monitor: tier
  share of session time, with attention to the trend, not just the level.
- **Beginner overload.** Toggling full complexity reintroduces the cycle-2
  problem. Mitigation: buried placement plus comprehension barrier. Monitor:
  beginner toggling and accuracy.
- **Friction signal.** Heavy custom-mode use may indicate the tiers themselves
  grind too much, a question to investigate about the core path.

### 12. Alternatives Considered

| Option | Why not |
| --- | --- |
| Backward-only tier revisiting | Lets players rehearse basics but not reach hard complexity without grinding. |
| Free forward movement | Destroys earned progression and the anti-overwhelm gating at once. |
| Fully split tiers + recommended tabs | Collapses the progression spine into a menu and relies on a nudge users ignore. |
| Separate custom-practice mode (chosen) | Serves stronger players via a parallel surface while leaving progression fully intact. |

</PRDDoc>

## Prioritization

<TLDR>Chose a separate custom-practice mode running parallel to the tiers, so progression is untouched because practice and progression are now two different surfaces.</TLDR>

Each rejected option protected one thing by sacrificing another:

- **Backward-only revisiting** (replay a tier you've cleared, no skipping ahead).
  Preserves progression integrity, but does nothing for the advanced player who
  wants to drill a *hard* variable now without grinding up to it.
- **Free forward movement** through tiers. Solves access, but destroys the
  earned-progression feeling *and* the anti-overwhelm gating in one move.
- **Fully split the tiers** (choose any tier freely, with "recommended" tabs to
  guide placement). Flexible, but it collapses the single progression spine into a
  menu and leans on a recommendation nudge users ignore. Cycle 2 already taught us
  that users don't change settings they should.
- **Custom practice as a separate mode (chosen).** A "custom practice" option at
  the bottom of the skill-selection screen where you toggle on one or multiple
  variables to build the exact environment you want.

**The insight:** don't modify the progression path. *Add a parallel one.* The
tiers stay pristine as the structured spine, and custom mode is a sandbox beside
it. No one's progression is touched, because practice and progression are now two
different surfaces. Placing it at the *bottom* of skill selection is deliberate:
discoverable for players who seek it, off the default path for a beginner who
shouldn't.

breaking the progression system the tiers exist to create. The guardrails carry as
much weight as the primary.

**Primary, trial vs. retained:** we discount the 27 of 40 who *tried* custom mode.
It launched bundled with other new features, so trial measures curiosity, not
value. Retained use is the number that counts, and the people who needed it
adopted it while beginners largely didn't.

**Guardrail 1, read two-sided:** structured-tier share of session time moved from
97% to 86%, roughly an 11-point shift into custom practice.

- *Reassuring:* 86% of time is still in the progression spine, so custom mode is a
  supplement, not a replacement.
- *Skeptical:* an 11-point drain in a single early window on a brand-new feature.
  The risk is the *slope*, not the level. Does it stabilize near 86%, or keep
  bleeding as novelty fades and advanced players realize they can live in the
  sandbox? One window can't tell us. Honest claim: held so far, watching the
  trend, not "the tiers held."

**Guardrail 2, beginner overload:** no observed beginner toggling or misuse. The
buried placement (off the default path) plus the comprehension barrier (beginners
don't yet understand the variables well enough to over-toggle) both did their job.
Caveat: small, early window, worth tighter instrumentation before calling it
permanently safe.

**Secondary and diagnostic signals worth instrumenting:**

- Which variable *combinations* get toggled most (a direct roadmap signal for what
  players actually want to drill).
- Whether custom-mode users show *faster* tier progression on the main path (the
  optimistic hypothesis: targeted practice feeds structured improvement).

### Retro & lessons

**The honest second-order read:** an 11-point shift toward the sandbox might also
be telling us the *tiers themselves* are more of a grind than we'd like, and
advanced players are routing around friction. That is not necessarily bad, but it
is a question the data raises and doesn't answer, and it is about the rest of the
product, not just this feature.

**Lesson:** when a structured system frustrates a subset of users, the move isn't
to loosen the structure for everyone. It is to add an optional parallel surface for
the users who need it, and protect the structure with a guardrail you actually
watch.

## Summary (BLUF)

In the first week, too many onboarded free-tier users never added a watch, which
is the core activation action for that tier. I shipped two things to attack the
two distinct frictions behind non-adding: a guided onboarding demo of the add
flow, and a recommended-adds section driven by community demand and US-to-Japan
margin signals. The free-tier watchlist-add rate reached 95%, beating the 85%
target, and recommendations were involved in about a third of adds.

## Trigger & problem

<TLDR>In week one only ~220 of ~280 onboarded free-tier users added a watch, and for a collector-only tier the watchlist add is the true activation signal.</TLDR>

The first-week funnel ran roughly 360 users → 280 who completed onboarding → 220
who added a watch. Watchlist-add is the activation signal for the free tier. The
free tier has no trader functionality and exposes collector data only, so every
free-tier user is a collector for whom a watchlist is the correct first action.
That makes add-rate a true activation metric for this segment, and too many users
were stopping short of it.

## Discovery & user-research synthesis

<TLDR>Working backward from a non-adder surfaced two distinct frictions: selection (which model to track) and procedural (never actually walked through the add).</TLDR>

Working backward from a non-adder's experience surfaced two distinct frictions:

- **Selection.** Users didn't know which exact model or reference to track.
- **Procedural.** Onboarding showed where and how to add, but never walked the user
  through actually doing it.

Two different problems meant a single fix wouldn't be enough.

## The PRD

<TLDR>Attack both frictions: a guided onboarding demo of the add flow, plus a recommended-adds section, without auto-adding anything.</TLDR>

### 1. Summary
In week one, too many onboarded free-tier users never added a watch, the core
activation action for that tier. This PRD adds a guided onboarding demo of the add
flow and a recommended-adds section (community demand plus US-to-Japan margin and
price signals) to attack the two distinct frictions behind non-adding. Goal: lift
free-tier watchlist-add rate to at least 85%.

### 2. Problem & Background
First-week funnel: ~360 users → ~280 completed onboarding → ~220 added a watch.
Watchlist-add is the activation signal for free tier. Because the free tier has no
trader functionality (collector data only), every free-tier user is a collector
for whom a watchlist is the correct first action, so add-rate is a true activation
metric for this segment.

### 3. Discovery
Working backward from a non-adder's experience surfaced two distinct frictions:

- Selection: users don't know which exact model or reference to track.
- Procedural: onboarding showed where and how to add but didn't walk users through
  actually doing it.

### 4. Goals & Non-Goals
**Goals**

- Lift the share of free-tier users who take the core activation action.
- Reduce both selection and procedural friction.
- Preserve the short onboarding and the meaningfulness of adds.

**Non-Goals**

- Auto-adding a watch on the user's behalf.
- Changing trader-tier flows.

### 5. Success Metrics
(Primary set before launch.)

- **Primary, free-tier watchlist-add rate:** at least 85%. (Result: 163 of 172 =
  95%.)
- **Attribution, source of add:** reference-only, recommendation-only, or both.
  (Result: 104, 13, 46. Recommendation-involved = 59, about 36%.)
- **Guardrail 1, add meaningfulness:** retention and engagement of added watches.
  (Lagging metric for this niche. About 10 of 163 removed in week one.
  Return-engagement is the truer signal.)
- **Guardrail 2, onboarding completion:** must not drop from the added demo step.
  (Result: held.)

### 6. Target User
Free-tier collectors, users with collector intent and no trader functionality, for
whom building a watchlist is the natural first action.

### 7. User Stories

- As a new collector, I want to be shown how to add a watch during onboarding, so
  I'm not guessing at the mechanics.
- As a new collector who isn't sure which watch to track first, I want relevant
  suggestions, so I have a concrete starting point.
- As a user, I don't want the product adding watches I didn't choose.

### 8. Solution Overview

- **Onboarding demo (procedural friction):** onboarding shows an actual on-screen
  example of entering a reference number and clicking add. It is demonstrated, but
  it does not perform the add for the user.
- **Recommended adds (selection friction):** a section below the watchlist screen
  surfacing recommended watches based on what other users are adding and what is
  declining in price or rising in US-to-Japan margin.

### 9. Functional Requirements

- Onboarding includes a non-executing demonstration of the add flow.
- The watchlist screen renders a recommended-adds section ranked by demand plus
  margin and price signals.
- Each add records its source (manual reference or recommendation) for attribution.

### 10. Edge Cases & Constraints

- No recommendations available (sparse data or a brand-new user): needs a defined
  fallback, either a generic starter set or hiding the section.
- Recommended watch already on the user's list: suppress it or mark it as added.
- Constraint: must not lengthen onboarding enough to hurt completion.

### 11. Risks & Mitigations

- **Hollow adds.** Recommendations could juice adds with watches users don't value,
  the reason auto-add was rejected. Monitor: add retention and return-engagement,
  on a months-long horizon appropriate to the niche.
- **Onboarding friction.** A demo step could reduce completion. Mitigation and
  monitor: watch the completion rate (result: held).
- **Attribution.** Two fixes shipped together limit isolation. Mitigation: the
  source-of-add split attributes the recommendation feature directly. The demo's
  separate effect is acknowledged as not fully isolable.

### 12. Alternatives Considered

| Option | Why not |
| --- | --- |
| Auto-add a sample watch during onboarding | Most users delete it immediately, a hollow add that clutters the list and games the metric. |
| Show-only onboarding (where and how, no demo) | The status quo that produced the gap. Explains but doesn't teach the motion. |
| Onboarding demo + recommended adds (chosen) | Addresses procedural and selection friction directly, without faking the add. |

## Prioritization

<TLDR>Chose a demo plus recommendations over auto-adding a sample watch, which would only create hollow adds that game the metric.</TLDR>

I considered three options:

- **Auto-add a sample watch during onboarding.** It would lift the number, but most
  users delete it immediately. That is a hollow add that clutters the list and
  games the metric rather than activating anyone.
- **Show-only onboarding (where and how, no demo).** This was the status quo, and it
  is what produced the gap. It explains the add but doesn't teach the motion.
- **Onboarding demo plus recommended adds (chosen).** It addresses the procedural
  and selection frictions directly, without faking the add.

**The insight:** don't fake the activation action. Two distinct frictions needed
two distinct fixes, and the honest version removes the friction so the user takes
the real action rather than manufacturing the metric.

## Build & sprint summary

<TLDR>Shipped two surfaces: a non-executing add-flow demo in onboarding, and a recommended-adds section ranked by community demand and US-to-Japan margin signals.</TLDR>

Two surfaces:

- **Onboarding demo (procedural friction).** Onboarding shows an on-screen example
  of entering a reference number and clicking add. It is demonstrated, but it
  deliberately does not perform the add for the user.
- **Recommended adds (selection friction).** A section below the watchlist surfaces
  recommended watches based on what other users are adding and what is declining in
  price or rising in US-to-Japan margin.

Each add records its source, manual reference or recommendation, so the
recommendation feature can be attributed directly.

**Attribution:** each add recorded its source. Of the 163 adds, 104 came from a
reference only, 13 from a recommendation only, and 46 from both. Recommendations
were involved in 59 adds, about 36%. That attributes the recommendation feature
directly. Because two fixes shipped together, the demo's separate effect is
acknowledged as not fully isolable.

**Hollow-add risk:** recommendations could juice adds with watches users don't
actually value, which is exactly why auto-add was rejected. The real test is add
retention and return-engagement, on a months-long horizon appropriate to this
niche. In week one only about 10 of 163 adds were removed, which is reassuring but
early.

### Retro & lessons

Don't fake the activation action. Two distinct frictions needed two distinct fixes,
and the honest move is to attribute what you can (the recommendation split) while
naming what you can't isolate (the demo that shipped alongside it).
